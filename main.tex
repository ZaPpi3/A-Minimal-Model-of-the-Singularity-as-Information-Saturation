\begin{abstract}
Classical general relativity predicts singularities where curvature diverges. 
Modern quantum-gravity models instead replace these divergences with finite 
transition points. This paper presents a minimal interpretation of the singularity 
as the moment when a collapsing spacetime reaches its maximum information density. 
At this point all degrees of freedom have been reduced to their minimal binary 
units, saturating the holographic entropy bound. Further compression is impossible, 
and the interior undergoes a bounce into a new spacetime region.
\end{abstract}

\section*{1. Idea}
A collapsing black hole compresses matter and fields. As the radius decreases, 
the information density increases. The holographic principle limits the total 
information to


\[
I_{\max} = \frac{A_{\text{app}}}{4L_{P}^{2}},
\]


where $A_{\text{app}}$ is the area of the \emph{apparent} (dynamical) horizon. 
This horizon is quasi-local and evolves with infalling matter, so the interior 
is constrained by a local, not global, entropy bound.

The quantity $I$ denotes the coarse-grained information content of the collapsing 
interior, defined as the effective number of independent degrees of freedom 
remaining after gravitational collapse erases longitudinal structure. Both $I$ 
and $I_{\max}$ scale with the transverse area, ensuring a consistent comparison.

\section*{2. Singularity as a Limit State}
The singularity is redefined as the moment when


\[
I = I_{\max}.
\]


At this stage all physical degrees of freedom have been reduced to binary form 
(0/1). This is a finite, physical limit rather than a divergence.

Because collapse eliminates one dimension of freedom, the remaining independent 
degrees of freedom scale as


\[
I \propto r^{2},
\]


matching the holographic capacity


\[
I_{\max} \propto A_{\text{app}} \propto r^{2}.
\]


Thus the saturation condition is naturally reached at the minimum radius.

\section*{3. Bounce}
At the minimum radius $r_{0}$, the apparent-horizon area scales as 
$A_{\text{app}} \propto r_{0}^{2}$, fixing the holographic capacity 
$I_{\max} \propto r_{0}^{2}$, while the interior density scales as 
$\rho \propto M/r_{0}^{3}$. Because gravitational collapse eliminates 
longitudinal structure, the remaining independent degrees of freedom 
scale as $I \propto r_{0}^{2}$, so that at $r = r_{0}$ the interior 
information content exactly saturates the holographic bound 
($I = I_{\max}$) at the same moment the critical density 
$\rho = \rho_{c}$ is reached.

The modified Friedmann equation,


\[
H^{2} = \alpha \rho \left(1 - \frac{\rho}{\rho_{c}}\right),
\]


forces $H \rightarrow 0$ at $\rho = \rho_{c}$. Further collapse would require 
exceeding the holographic entropy bound, which is forbidden. Quantum-gravity 
corrections therefore reverse the sign of the effective gravitational dynamics, 
producing a bounce in which the interior transitions from contraction to 
expansion. The bounce is thus the dynamical response of spacetime to 
information saturation.

\section*{4. Summary}
The singularity is reinterpreted as an information-saturation point: the final 
stage of collapse where all structure has been reduced to binary units and no 
further compression is possible. This provides a simple, non-singular description 
of black-hole interiors and aligns naturally with quantum-gravity bounce models.
